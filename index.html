Regularized Latent Dynamics Prediction

RLDP is a simple representation learning baseline for zero-shot reinforcement learning. It predicts latent future state evolution and uses orthogonality regularization to prevent mild feature collapse, yielding features that are both predictive and broadly useful downstream.

Motivation

How far can a simple predictive objective really go?

Zero-shot RL needs representations that are not tied to one task or one reward. RLDP studies whether latent next-state prediction, one of the simplest temporal learning signals available, can already serve as a strong foundation model objective.

A strong baseline is valuable

Rather than starting from a heavy objective, RLDP starts with a minimal one and asks what is missing. That framing makes the method useful scientifically: it reveals which ingredients are essential for zero-shot performance and which are not.

Predictive features should encode how states evolve over time, not just how they look.
Those same features must remain broad enough to support many downstream rewards.
A simple method that performs well is easier to interpret, compare, and build upon.

The central question

Does latent next-state prediction produce state features that enable performant zero-shot reinforcement learning? RLDP shows that the answer is almost yes by default, and clearly yes once mild collapse is controlled with the right regularization.

Predictive Features track future state evolution.

Core Insight

Naive latent prediction quietly drives features toward similarity.

The issue is not that latent dynamics prediction fails. The issue is that, in its naive form, it can produce a mild form of feature collapse. RLDP addresses that failure mode directly with orthogonality regularization.

What is collapsing?

The representation still predicts something useful, but too many directions in feature space start to resemble each other. That weakens the representation as a general-purpose substrate for many possible rewards.

Prediction quality alone does not guarantee a well-shaped representation.
Feature similarity is a geometric warning sign for downstream expressiveness.
Orthogonality regularization preserves diversity without abandoning the predictive signal.
Method

Predict the future, regularize the geometry.

RLDP learns representations from offline interaction data by predicting latent next states. An orthogonality regularizer then keeps the learned features from collapsing into overly similar directions.

Overview of RLDP

The method is intentionally compact: encode observations, model future latent evolution, and shape the representation space so the resulting features stay diverse enough for broad zero-shot control.

Three ingredients

RLDP stays simple enough to interpret while still capturing the ingredients that matter most.

  • Latent prediction Learn a state representation grounded in temporal evolution.
  • Orthogonality regularization Prevent mild collapse by preserving informative directions in feature space.
  • Frozen downstream use Evaluate the representation through zero-shot reinforcement learning tasks.
Why RLDP

A baseline that is easy to trust and hard to ignore.

RLDP is attractive not just because it works, but because it works with an objective that is simple, readable, and scientifically revealing.

Simple objective

Latent dynamics prediction is a natural temporal signal and avoids unnecessary objective complexity.

Better representation geometry

Orthogonality regularization protects feature diversity, which improves transfer to a broad family of rewards.

Strong low-coverage behavior

RLDP remains particularly effective when offline datasets become less forgiving and coverage drops.

Results

Competitive across offline, online, and low-coverage settings.

Across benchmarks, RLDP consistently shows that once mild collapse is handled, a simple predictive representation objective becomes a strong baseline for behavioral foundation models.

Offline Zero-Shot RL

On DeepMind Control Suite tasks with ExoRL datasets, RLDP is competitive with strong prior representation learning methods while maintaining a much simpler training objective.

Benchmarks include Pointmass, Cheetah, Walker, and Quadruped.

Online Zero-Shot RL

In the high-dimensional SMPL humanoid setting, frozen RLDP representations support competitive multi-task policy learning once the agent is allowed online interaction.

The violin plot shows RLDP is often competitive and sometimes better than baseline methods.

Low-Coverage Datasets

RLDP is especially compelling when offline data coverage is limited, outperforming baseline methods on many D4RL MuJoCo tasks where representation quality becomes especially brittle.

A large fraction of the violin mass above zero indicates broad gains over comparison methods.

Toward World Modeling

RLDP points toward a broader lesson for predictive models.

Predictive objectives are powerful, but representation geometry matters. RLDP suggests that future world models and behavior foundation models may benefit from simple predictive cores paired with explicit controls on collapse and diversity.

Why this matters beyond one benchmark

The project highlights a clean design principle: predictive learning should not only model what happens next, but also preserve the structure needed for reuse. That makes RLDP interesting as more than a baseline; it is a hint about how to scale predictive representation learning responsibly.

Temporal prediction can be a strong backbone for foundation models in control.
Regularization of representation geometry may be a key companion objective.
Low-coverage robustness is especially relevant for realistic offline data regimes.

The take-home message

RLDP is compelling precisely because it does not rely on a complicated stack of ingredients. It shows that a well-regularized predictive objective can already serve as a credible, strong foundation for zero-shot RL.

Predictive enough to encode state evolution.
Diverse enough to support many rewards.
Simple enough to be a meaningful baseline.
