Regularized Latent Dynamics Prediction is a Strong Baseline for Behavioral Foundation Models

RLDP asks a simple question with surprisingly strong consequences: can next-state prediction alone produce representations useful for zero-shot reinforcement learning? The answer is yes, if we prevent the representation from collapsing and make diversity a first-class objective.

Core insight

Prediction alone is not enough. Predictive and diverse is the real target.

RLDP shows that naive latent dynamics prediction can drift toward a mild form of feature collapse. The regularized variant keeps the representation expressive, preserving structure that can support a broader family of downstream rewards without losing predictive power.

Why the baseline matters

Strong behavior foundation models do not always require elaborate objectives. RLDP intentionally keeps the learning signal simple, then asks what minimal ingredient is needed to make it robust for zero-shot RL.

Naive next-state prediction can over-concentrate features and reduce coverage of useful directions in representation space.
A diversity regularizer counteracts collapse while still keeping features predictive of future state evolution.
The resulting representation transfers well across benchmark settings, including lower-coverage offline datasets.

What changes with regularization

The regularizer does not replace the predictive objective. Instead, it reshapes the geometry of the learned space so RLDP can encode futures that remain both discriminative and reusable for zero-shot control.

Future evolution is still encoded through dynamics prediction in latent space.
Representation diversity improves the usefulness of those features for many rewards, not just one narrow objective.
The method remains simple enough to serve as a clean, credible baseline for behavioral foundation models.
Method

How RLDP works

The representation is learned from offline interaction data through latent next-state prediction, then improved with a regularizer that explicitly protects the diversity of the embedding space.

Overview of the learning pipeline

RLDP uses a compact idea: encode observations, predict how those latent states evolve, and regularize the geometry of the features so they remain broadly useful for reward-conditioned downstream control.

Design principles

The method is intentionally lightweight, making it easier to interpret what each ingredient contributes.

  • Predict future state evolution Learn a representation grounded in how the environment changes over time.
  • Guard against collapse Encourage the embedding space to retain multiple informative directions.
  • Transfer without task labels Evaluate the frozen representation through zero-shot reinforcement learning downstream.
Results

Competitive performance across the settings that matter

RLDP is evaluated in offline zero-shot RL, online zero-shot RL, and low-coverage dataset settings. Across these experiments, the message is consistent: a simple predictive baseline becomes strong when its representation geometry is handled carefully.

Offline Zero-Shot RL

On DeepMind Control Suite tasks with ExoRL data, RLDP remains competitive with state-of-the-art representation learning baselines while keeping the training objective simple and interpretable.

Benchmarks include Pointmass, Cheetah, Walker, and Quadruped.

Online Zero-Shot RL

In the high-dimensional SMPL humanoid setting, frozen RLDP representations enable strong multi-task policy learning when the agent is later allowed online interaction.

Evaluation spans 45 tasks, with the violin plot showing RLDP is frequently competitive or better.

Low-Coverage Datasets

RLDP stays effective when the offline data distribution becomes less forgiving, outperforming baseline methods on many D4RL MuJoCo tasks where representation quality is especially sensitive to coverage.

The mass of the violin above zero indicates broad advantages over comparison methods.
RLDP highlights how a carefully regularized predictive objective can become a reliable baseline for zero-shot reinforcement learning.
